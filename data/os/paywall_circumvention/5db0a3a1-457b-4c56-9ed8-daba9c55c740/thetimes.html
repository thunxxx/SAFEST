The announcement came as a surprise to many in Westminster, with senior figures from both major parties expressing caution about the potential implications. The proposed legislation, which has been in development for several months, aims to address long-standing concerns over regulatory oversight in the financial sector.

According to documents seen by The Times, the new framework would grant additional powers to the regulatory authority, including the ability to impose stricter penalties for non-compliance. Industry experts have described the move as "the most significant reform in a decade."

Critics, however, argue that the changes could stifle innovation and place an undue burden on smaller firms. "We need to strike a balance between accountability and growth," said one senior banking executive who spoke on condition of anonymity. "This feels like a sledgehammer to crack a nut."

The Treasury has defended the proposals, stating that they are necessary to maintain the UK's reputation as a global financial hub. A spokesperson said: "These measures are designed to protect consumers and ensure the integrity of our markets. We have consulted widely and believe this is a proportionate response."

Meanwhile, opposition parties have called for further scrutiny, with the shadow chancellor demanding a full parliamentary debate before any legislation is introduced. The debate is expected to intensify in the coming weeks as more details emerge.

In related news, the Bank of England has indicated that it will monitor the situation closely, though it stopped short of endorsing the specific proposals. Economists are divided on the potential impact, with some predicting increased stability and others warning of unintended consequences.
