Quantum Neural Architectures for Unsupervised Cross-Modal Learning in High-Energy Physics

Abstract
The integration of quantum computing principles with neural network architectures offers transformative potential for analyzing complex high-energy physics datasets. Here we present a novel quantum-inspired neural framework that performs unsupervised cross-modal learning across detector signatures, simulation data, and theoretical models. Our architecture achieves a 37.8% improvement in anomaly detection sensitivity compared to classical deep learning baselines on the CERN Open Data portal, while reducing computational requirements by orders of magnitude for specific tensor operations. We demonstrate the framework's capability to identify rare decay processes without labeled training data, opening new avenues for discovery in particle physics.

1. Introduction

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN produces petabytes of collision data annually, presenting both unprecedented opportunities and formidable challenges for data analysis. Traditional supervised learning methods require extensive labeled datasets, which are often unavailable for rare or theoretical processes. Unsupervised learning, particularly cross-modal approaches that leverage multiple data representations simultaneously, has emerged as a promising alternative. However, classical neural networks face fundamental limitations in capturing the high-dimensional correlations inherent in particle physics data.

Quantum computing offers a paradigm shift in representational capacity. Recent theoretical work suggests that quantum neural networks can efficiently approximate functions that are classically intractable. Here, we bridge this gap by introducing a hybrid quantum-classical architecture designed specifically for unsupervised cross-modal learning in high-energy physics.

2. Related Work

Previous efforts in anomaly detection for particle physics have employed autoencoders, variational autoencoders (VAEs), and generative adversarial networks (GANs). Farina et al. (2020) demonstrated the use of VAEs for jet tagging, while Heimel et al. (2021) introduced a weakly supervised approach using classification without labels. In the quantum domain, Henderson et al. (2020) proposed quantum kernel methods for classification tasks, but their applicability to unsupervised cross-modal settings remained unexplored. Our work extends these foundations by introducing a tensor network layer that operates natively in a quantum-inspired Hilbert space.

3. Methodology

3.1 Quantum Tensor Layer

At the core of our architecture is the Quantum Tensor Layer (QTL), which implements a parameterized quantum circuit operating on 8 qubits. The QTL maps classical input vectors to a high-dimensional Hilbert space via amplitude encoding. We employ a hardware-efficient ansatz with alternating layers of single-qubit rotations and entangling CNOT gates. The output is obtained by measuring expectation values of Pauli operators, yielding a feature vector of dimension 64.

|ψ(x)⟩ = U(θ) · |x⟩,   f(x) = ⟨ψ(x)|O|ψ(x)⟩

The QTL is trained end-to-end using a hybrid gradient computation: parameter-shift rules for quantum parameters and standard backpropagation for classical layers. This allows seamless integration with classical neural components.

3.2 Cross-Modal Contrastive Learning

We adopt a contrastive learning objective that maximizes mutual information between different modalities of the same physical event. For each event, we have three views: calorimeter energy deposits (E), tracker momentum vectors (P), and muon chamber hits (M). The loss function is defined as:

L = -log( exp(sim(z_E, z_P)/τ) / Σ exp(sim(z_E, z_j)/τ) ) - log( exp(sim(z_E, z_M)/τ) / Σ exp(sim(z_E, z_j)/τ) )

where z represents the projected embeddings and τ is a temperature parameter. This formulation encourages the network to learn representations that are invariant to the measurement modality while preserving event-specific information.

4. Experimental Results (Premium Content)

We evaluated our framework on the CERN Open Data portal, specifically using the 2012 CMS dataset comprising 10,000 simulated events of Higgs boson decays to tau leptons (H→ττ) and 100,000 background events from QCD multijet processes. The dataset includes detector-level information from three subsystems: electromagnetic calorimeter (ECAL), hadronic calorimeter (HCAL), and the muon system.

Our quantum neural architecture achieved an area under the ROC curve (AUC) of 0.942 ± 0.008 for anomaly detection, compared to 0.684 for a classical VAE baseline and 0.723 for a classical contrastive learning model. The improvement was particularly pronounced in low-statistics regions where classical methods struggle due to limited training examples.

Computational efficiency analysis revealed that the QTL reduces the number of parameters by a factor of 16 compared to a fully connected layer of equivalent expressivity, while the tensor network contraction operations can be parallelized on classical hardware using GPU-accelerated libraries. End-to-end training converged in 47 epochs, compared to 120+ epochs for classical counterparts.

5. Discussion and Implications

The results demonstrate that quantum-inspired tensor networks provide a powerful inductive bias for high-energy physics data. The cross-modal contrastive objective naturally exploits the multi-detector nature of LHC experiments, learning representations that are robust to detector inefficiencies and systematic uncertainties. Importantly, the unsupervised nature of our approach means it can be applied directly to collision data without requiring simulated training labels, making it suitable for real-time anomaly detection at the trigger level.

We anticipate that future implementations on actual quantum hardware could yield further speedups for specific subroutines, particularly for the tensor contraction operations that scale polynomially in the bond dimension but remain challenging for classical computers at scale.

5.1 Limitations

Current quantum hardware limitations restrict the QTL to 8-12 qubits, corresponding to a Hilbert space dimension of 256-4096. While sufficient for proof-of-concept demonstrations, scaling to full detector granularity will require either larger quantum processors or classical approximations of the quantum layer. Additionally, the amplitude encoding scheme is sensitive to input normalization and requires careful preprocessing.

6. Conclusion and Future Work

We have presented a quantum neural architecture for unsupervised cross-modal learning that achieves state-of-the-art anomaly detection performance on high-energy physics data. The framework's ability to learn from raw detector data without labels represents a significant step toward autonomous scientific discovery in particle physics. Future work will explore integration with real-time data acquisition systems and extension to other domains such as astrophysics and materials science.
