The great unbundling: How trade is being reshaped by geopolitics and technology

For decades, the prevailing wisdom held that global trade would continue to expand, driven by ever-lower barriers and ever-more efficient supply chains. That era is now ending. A new pattern is emerging, one defined not by integration but by fragmentation, as national security concerns and technological shifts redraw the map of commerce.

The numbers tell a stark story. After peaking at around 60% of global GDP in 2008, trade in goods and services has plateaued. More recently, it has begun to decline in real terms when measured against overall economic output. This is not merely a cyclical downturn; it reflects structural changes in the world economy.

One of the most significant drivers is the weaponisation of economic interdependence. The United States and China, once each other’s largest trading partners, are now actively decoupling. Tariffs, export controls, and investment screening have become tools of statecraft, forcing companies to choose sides.

Meanwhile, technology is enabling a different kind of fragmentation. Digital services, cloud computing, and AI allow companies to separate production from consumption in unprecedented ways. A car designed in Germany, with software from India, batteries from China, and assembly in Mexico, is now the norm rather than the exception.

What does this mean for businesses and policymakers? The era of "just-in-time" global supply chains is giving way to "just-in-case" redundancy. Companies are building buffer stocks, dual-sourcing critical components, and reshoring production of essential goods. This is expensive, but in a world of sanctions and disruptions, it has become a necessary insurance policy.

The World Trade Organisation, once the arbiter of global commerce, has been sidelined. Its dispute-resolution mechanism is broken, and major powers are increasingly bypassing multilateral frameworks in favour of bilateral deals and technology clubs. The result is a patchwork of rules, not a single system.

Yet there are also opportunities. Countries that can navigate this new landscape—by investing in digital infrastructure, building resilient supply chains, and forging smart partnerships—may emerge stronger. The winners will not be the largest economies, but the most adaptable ones.
